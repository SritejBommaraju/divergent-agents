# Benchmarking methodology — papers, protocols & honesty traps

*The verified evidence base behind [`../BENCHMARKING.md`](../BENCHMARKING.md). 25 papers, 25 confirmed real. Re-verification ledger appended by `bench/recheck_methodology.py`.*


## Objective semantic-distance scoring of divergent thinking

**Recommended protocol**

1. 1. Pre-register the task, prompts, sampling settings (temperature, top-p), number of samples per prompt, and ALL scoring functions before running any system — freeze them in a public config so nothing is tuned after seeing results.
2. 2. Use a no-judge objective core: score divergence as mean pairwise embedding distance over a FROZEN ensemble of public semantic spaces (SemDis-style composite), with exact model versions pinned. No LLM grades the output in this core metric.
3. 3. Always report two numbers, never one: (a) per-response originality (distance of an output from the prompt/obvious answer) AND (b) population diversity (mean pairwise distance ACROSS many sampled outputs from the same prompt, plus distinct-n / unique-response rate). Per Wenger & Kenett, (a) can be high while (b) collapses.
4. 4. Control the confounds before interpreting: strip stop words, normalize or covary out response length/elaboration and fluency (idea count), and report scores both raw and length-controlled. If the effect vanishes after length control, it was verbosity, not novelty.
5. 5. Anchor to a human reference distribution, not an absolute scale: collect (or reuse a public dataset of) human responses to the same prompts and report how far each system's diversity distribution sits relative to humans — semantic distance is only validated as a relative measure.
6. 6. Validate divergence against appropriateness so you reward useful novelty, not noise: gate or jointly report a feasibility/coherence check (random word salad maximizes distance but is not creative). Keep quality and diversity as separate axes (per Dhingra), never merged into one headline score.
7. 7. If you add a supervised 'rated originality' axis, use a scorer (e.g., Ocsai-style) fine-tuned on HUMAN judgments for THIS task, frozen, and never trained on the candidate systems' outputs — and treat it as a calibrated human-proxy, not an independent oracle. Disclose its inter-rater ceiling.
8. 8. Run sanity baselines: a verbose-paraphrase bot, a random-dictionary-word generator, and a fixed human sample. An honest metric should rank the random generator high on raw distance but LOW after appropriateness gating, and should not let the paraphrase bot beat humans.
9. 9. Report inter-metric and (where human ratings exist) metric-vs-human correlations, plus confidence intervals across prompts, so readers see where the objective metric agrees with humans and where it diverges.
10. 10. Release prompts, raw outputs, scoring code, and embedding-model versions for independent recomputation.

**Honesty traps (how this measurement can lie) & fixes**

- Grading your own homework: using a general LLM to judge which output is more 'creative' — it imports the model's own stylistic preferences and can favor sibling models. Avoid by making the core metric a no-judge embedding distance; if you use a learned scorer, it must be human-supervised, frozen, and never trained on the candidates' outputs.
- Verbosity/elaboration inflation: longer responses mechanically gain embedding distance and win LLM-judge comparisons. Avoid by stripping stop words, normalizing/covarying out length, and reporting raw vs length-controlled scores (Beaty et al. 2022).
- Fluency confound: generating MORE ideas raises aggregate distance independent of quality. Avoid by scoring per-item, not aggregate, and partialling out idea count.
- Per-response originality hiding diversity collapse: a system scores high on each single output yet produces a narrow, repetitive distribution. Avoid by always reporting cross-sample population diversity (mean pairwise distance across many samples, distinct-n) against a human reference (Wenger & Kenett 2025).
- Embedding cherry-pick: choosing the one semantic space (or hosted API snapshot) that flatters your system; results then also drift as the API updates. Avoid by pre-registering a frozen public ensemble with pinned versions (SemDis).
- Distance without appropriateness = rewarding gibberish: random or incoherent words maximize semantic distance but aren't creative. Avoid by gating/jointly reporting a feasibility/coherence check and including a random-word baseline that should fail it.
- Lexical/paraphrase metrics rewarding surface change: n-gram or token-overlap 'diversity' rewards reworded restatements of the obvious answer. Avoid by scoring in meaning space (embeddings) and including a paraphrase-bot baseline that must not beat humans.
- Single headline 'diversity' number: diversity is task- and objective-relative and can be inflated by sacrificing correctness or safety. Avoid by keeping quality and diversity as separate, jointly-reported axes and naming which diversity you measure (Dhingra 2026).
- Treating semantic distance as an absolute creativity scale: it is only validated as a relative, human-anchored correlate. Avoid by always benchmarking against a human distribution rather than claiming an absolute 'novelty' value.
- Prompt/seed leakage and post-hoc tuning: adjusting temperature, prompts, or metric weights after seeing scores. Avoid by pre-registration and releasing the full pipeline for independent recomputation.

**Papers**

- ✅ [Naming unrelated words predicts creativity (the Divergent Association Task)](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2022340118) — *Jay A. Olson, Johnny Nahas, Denis Chmoulevitch, Simon J. Cropper, Margaret E. Webb* (2021, Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS))  
  **Finding.** Asking people to name 10 maximally-unrelated nouns and scoring the mean pairwise GloVe cosine distance (the DAT) correlates with established creativity tasks (AUT, etc.) at least as strongly as those tasks correlate with each other, with good test-retest reliability — and the scoring is fully automatic, sample-independent, and requires no human or LLM judge.  
  **Use.** This is the canonical example of an HONEST objective divergence score: the metric is a fixed embedding-distance function with no grader in the loop, so it cannot be gamed by a model judging its own output. Use the DAT protocol (mean pairwise distance over a fixed-length set, deduplicated, valid dictionary words only) as the reference design for a no-judge divergence benchmark, and cite its validity ceiling — semantic distance only ever matched, never beat, human raters.  
  *Verified via Semantic Scholar search; DOI 10.1073/pnas.2022340118.*
- ✅ [Semantic Distance and the Alternate Uses Task: Recommendations for Reliable Automated Assessment of Originality](https://doi.org/10.1080/10400419.2022.2025720) — *Roger E. Beaty, Dan R. Johnson, Daniel C. Zeitlen, Boris Forthmann* (2022, Creativity Research Journal 34(3), 245-260)  
  **Finding.** Raw semantic-distance originality scores are confounded by response length/elaboration and by fluency (idea count): longer or more numerous responses mechanically inflate distance. They show the confound is attenuated by removing stop words, by item-level choices (avoid compound-word prompts, use many items), and by explicitly modeling/partialling out elaboration and fluency before interpreting distance as originality.  
  **Use.** This is the methodological backbone of an honest benchmark: it names the exact ways a distance metric LIES (verbosity and quantity masquerading as novelty) and gives the fixes — stop-word stripping, length normalization/covariate control, and per-item rather than aggregate scoring. Any divergence benchmark must report scores both raw and length-controlled, or it is measuring word count.  
  *Verified via Crossref DOI lookup 10.1080/10400419.2022.2025720.*
- ✅ [Automating creativity assessment with SemDis: An open platform for computing semantic distance](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13428-020-01453-w) — *Roger E. Beaty, Dan R. Johnson* (2021, Behavior Research Methods 53, 757-780)  
  **Finding.** No single semantic space is canonical: a composite that averages cosine distances across multiple embedding models (e.g., several GloVe/word2vec/LSA spaces) predicts human originality ratings more reliably than any individual model, because idiosyncratic errors of one space wash out in the mean.  
  **Use.** Guards against cherry-picking the embedding model that flatters your system. An honest benchmark should pre-register a fixed ensemble of public embedding spaces and report the composite, not the single best-correlating space — and should freeze model versions so results are reproducible rather than drifting with a hosted API.  
  *Verified via Crossref DOI lookup 10.3758/s13428-020-01453-w.*
- ✅ [Beyond semantic distance: Automated scoring of divergent thinking greatly improves with large language models (Ocsai)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tsc.2023.101356) — *Peter Organisciak, Selcuk Acar, Denis Dumas, Kelly Berthiaume* (2023, Thinking Skills and Creativity 49, 101356 (preprint: files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED629879.pdf))  
  **Finding.** Fine-tuning LLMs on human-judged AUT responses (Ocsai) reaches r up to ~.81 with human raters — near the human inter-rater ceiling and far above bag-of-words semantic distance — because distance models treat a response as independent words and ignore context/compositionality. But the model must be supervised on human judgments per task; it is a calibrated predictor of human ratings, not an independent oracle.  
  **Use.** Defines the honest ceiling and the honest danger. Honest use: a frozen, human-supervised scorer can be a second axis (rated originality) alongside no-judge distance — but only if it was trained on humans for THIS task and never on the candidate systems' own outputs. Dishonest use (avoid): letting a general LLM free-grade outputs, which is grading-your-own-homework and inherits the model's own stylistic preferences.  
  *Verified via Crossref DOI lookup 10.1016/j.tsc.2023.101356.*
- ✅ [We're Different, We're the Same: Creative Homogeneity Across LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.19361) — *Emily Wenger, Yoed N. Kenett* (2025, arXiv:2501.19361)  
  **Finding.** On standardized creativity tests, individual LLM responses can score as high-originality as humans, yet population-level diversity collapses: LLM responses are far more similar to OTHER LLMs' responses than humans are to each other, even after controlling for response structure. Per-item originality and population diversity are different quantities, and per-item scores hide the collapse.  
  **Use.** The single most important honesty correction for this axis: a system can win on mean per-response distance while producing a narrow, repetitive distribution. An honest 'thinks outside the box' benchmark must report a population/diversity metric (e.g., mean pairwise distance ACROSS many sampled outputs, distinct-n, coverage of the response space) — not just mean originality of one output — and compare against a human distribution as the reference.  
  *Verified via arXiv export API id_list=2501.19361.*
- ✅ [Magic, Madness, Heaven, Sin: LLM Output Diversity is Everything, Everywhere, All at Once](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.01504) — *Harnoor Dhingra* (2026, arXiv:2604.01504)  
  **Finding.** Argues output diversity is not a single model-intrinsic number: it is task- and objective-relative (epistemic/factual, interactional/utility, societal/representation, safety/robustness), and optimizing one objective (e.g., safety) can suppress creative diversity. A lone 'diversity score' is therefore underspecified and can mislead.  
  **Use.** Forces the benchmark to state WHICH diversity it claims and to keep quality and diversity as separate, jointly-reported axes rather than one headline number — so a system can't trade away correctness or coherence to inflate a diversity score and call it 'creativity'. Pair every divergence number with a validity/appropriateness check.  
  *Verified via arXiv export API id_list=2604.01504.*


## Novelty / diversity measurement protocols for LLM output

**Recommended protocol**

1. 1. Fix the elicitation protocol identically across systems: same prompt set (mix curated open-ended prompts a la NB-Curated with real user queries a la NB-WildChat), same k samples per prompt, same decoding seed policy, and a FIXED token budget per response so length cannot be traded for apparent quality or originality.
2. 2. Measure semantic diversity, not wording: cluster the k generations per prompt into functional-equivalence classes with a learned classifier (NoveltyBench's DeBERTa equivalence model) and also compute the Quality-Weighted Vendi Score with an embedding kernel. Report distinct_k (effective number of distinct ideas), not raw lexical variety.
3. 3. Measure originality against the actual training corpus, not a held-out reference: compute the unseen-n-gram fraction (4/5/6-grams) via an Infini-gram/WIMBD index so memorized regurgitation cannot masquerade as novelty.
4. 4. Measure quality with an INDEPENDENT judge from a different model family than the generator (avoid self-preference), score each cluster representative, and length-control the score (regress out length per AlpacaEval LC or hold length fixed).
5. 5. Combine, don't average naively: use a frontier/harmonic-style combiner (harmonic mean of originality and quality per output; NoveltyBench discounted utility_k across outputs) so a duplicate earns zero marginal credit and a novel-but-bad output earns zero — novelty only counts when it is also good.
6. 6. Report cheap surface metrics (Self-BLEU, distinct-n) as auditable sanity lower bounds alongside the semantic headline, and flag any case where lexical and semantic diversity disagree (a sign of paraphrase-gaming).
7. 7. Establish honest baselines and a human ceiling: include the inference-time tricks shown to be weak (raise temperature, 'be creative'/Denial prompting) as baselines the system must beat, and include human-written responses as the diversity ceiling.
8. 8. Run bias/robustness probes on the judge (CALM-style perturbations: position swap, verbosity injection, authority cues) and report the residual bias, so reviewers can trust the quality half of the score.
9. 9. Report variance: multiple seeds, confidence intervals on distinct_k/utility_k/Vendi, and per-prompt breakdowns — a single aggregate hides mode collapse on hard prompts.

**Honesty traps (how this measurement can lie) & fixes**

- Paraphrase laundering: lexical metrics (distinct-n, Self-BLEU) reward reworded duplicates as 'diverse'. Avoid by making the headline metric semantic — equivalence-class clustering and embedding-kernel Vendi — and using lexical metrics only as lower-bound sanity checks.
- Memorization disguised as novelty: judges/humans often score regurgitated training text highly, so quality-only metrics reward copying. Avoid by measuring originality as unseen-n-gram fraction against the real training corpus (Infini-gram), and combining via harmonic mean so memorized text can't score novel.
- Originality-without-quality noise: maximizing only originality (high temperature, rare tokens) yields incoherent gibberish that scores 'novel'. Avoid by the harmonic-mean/utility combiner that zeroes out low-quality outputs; never report a diversity number unconditioned on quality.
- Verbosity inflation: LLM judges (and n-gram originality) reward longer outputs regardless of added value. Avoid by length-controlling judge scores (AlpacaEval LC GLM) and fixing the token budget across systems.
- Grading your own homework (self-preference): a judge favors outputs from its own model family, inflating the evaluated system. Avoid by using a different-family judge, swapping generator/judge, and reporting human-validated agreement.
- Duplicate credit / metric saturation: averaged Self-BLEU and raw counts can be dominated by a few near-duplicates or saturate, hiding mode collapse. Avoid with Vendi (effective-mode count) and NoveltyBench's novelty-discounted utility that gives zero marginal credit to redundant outputs.
- Cherry-picked k and prompts: reporting only large k or only open-ended prompts inflates diversity. Avoid by fixing k in advance, including real-world user queries, and reporting per-prompt distributions with confidence intervals.
- Single-seed / single-metric reporting: diversity is high-variance and metric-specific. Avoid by multi-seed CIs and triangulating semantic + lexical + originality metrics; flag disagreements rather than reporting the most flattering one.
- Beating a strawman baseline: comparing against a deterministic/greedy baseline makes any sampling look diverse. Avoid by including strong baselines (temperature sweep, 'be creative' and Denial prompting) shown in the literature to barely move the frontier, plus a human ceiling.

**Papers**

- ✅ [NoveltyBench: Evaluating Language Models for Humanlike Diversity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.05228) — *Yiming Zhang, Harshita Diddee, Susan Holm, Hanchen Liu, Xinyue Liu, Vinay Samuel, Barry Wang, Daphne Ippolito* (2025, arXiv:2504.05228)  
  **Finding.** Defines diversity at the SEMANTIC (functional-equivalence) level, not the lexical level: k generations are partitioned into equivalence classes by a fine-tuned DeBERTa-v3-large classifier (trained on 1,000 human-annotated pairs; two outputs equivalent if 'a user who saw one would likely benefit from seeing the other'; 79% acc / 0.811 F1). distinct_k = number of unique classes. The headline metric utility_k = (1-p)/(1-p^k) * sum_i p^(i-1) * 1[c_i is new] * u_i, where p=0.8 is a user-patience discount, the indicator zeroes out redundant (duplicate-class) outputs, and u_i is a reward-model quality score (Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B) on a 1-10 scale. So a generation only earns credit if it is BOTH new and high-quality. Across 20 frontier models all show low diversity, and larger/more-capable models within a family are LESS diverse.  
  **Use.** Use utility_k as the core scoreboard: it operationalizes 'distinct AND good' in one number by giving zero marginal credit to a duplicate no matter how high its reward score, and zero credit to a novel-but-low-quality output. Adopt their two-stage protocol verbatim — (1) cluster samples by a learned functional-equivalence model, (2) score each cluster representative with an independent reward model, (3) aggregate with a discounted novelty-weighted utility. This is the right shape for benchmarking whether a system makes a model 'think outside the box': measure new equivalence classes surfaced, not new wordings.  
  *Verified via arxiv id_list lookup 2504.05228.*
- ✅ [Measuring LLM Novelty As The Frontier Of Original And High-Quality Output](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.09389) — *Vishakh Padmakumar, Chen Yueh-Han, Jane Pan, Valerie Chen, He He* (2025, arXiv:2504.09389)  
  **Finding.** Novelty must be the joint frontier of originality and quality, because each alone is gameable: originality alone rewards low-quality gibberish, and human/judge quality alone rewards memorized (regurgitated) text. They define novelty as the HARMONIC MEAN of (a) originality = fraction of 4/5/6-grams in the output that never appear in the model's pre- and post-training corpora (measured against the actual training data via Infini-gram / WIMBD indexes), and (b) quality (LLM-judge, renormalized to 0-1). The harmonic mean punishes a low value on either axis. Key result: scaling and post-training raise novelty mainly via quality; inference-time tricks (temperature, novelty prompting, Denial Prompting, novel ICL) barely shift the frontier — temperature buys originality but loses quality.  
  **Use.** Gives an honesty anchor against the single biggest trap — rewarding memorized regurgitation. To claim a system produces genuinely 'new' content, measure originality against the actual training corpus with an n-gram index (Infini-gram), not against a held-out reference set, and combine with quality via harmonic mean so neither axis can be traded away. Their negative result on inference-time methods is the benchmark baseline you must beat: any 'think-outside-the-box' system must move the originality-quality frontier, not just raise temperature.  
  *Verified via arxiv id_list lookup 2504.09389.*
- ✅ [The Vendi Score: A Diversity Evaluation Metric for Machine Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.02410) — *Dan Friedman, Adji Bousso Dieng* (2023, arXiv:2210.02410)  
  **Finding.** A reference-free diversity metric = exponential of the Shannon entropy of the eigenvalues of an n-by-n similarity matrix (built from any user-chosen similarity kernel over the samples). It reports an 'effective number of distinct modes' — e.g. 10 outputs that collapse to 2 themes score ~2, not 10. Crucially it needs NO reference distribution and is monotone in true diversity, so it does not saturate the way distinct-n does and is not fooled by a few near-duplicates the way averaged self-BLEU can be. Quality-Weighted Vendi extensions (2405.02449) fold per-sample quality into the same eigenvalue computation.  
  **Use.** Use Vendi (with an embedding-based similarity kernel) as the within-prompt diversity number that reports an interpretable effective-mode count, complementing NoveltyBench's discrete cluster count. Report Quality-Weighted Vendi so diversity credit is conditioned on quality. Because it is reference-free and similarity-function-agnostic, you can swap the kernel (semantic embedding vs. structural) to prove diversity isn't merely lexical reshuffling.  
  *Verified via arxiv id_list lookup 2210.02410.*
- ✅ [Length-Controlled AlpacaEval: A Simple Way to Debias Automatic Evaluators](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.04475) — *Yann Dubois, Balázs Galambosi, Percy Liang, Tatsunori B. Hashimoto* (2024, arXiv:2404.04475)  
  **Finding.** LLM-as-a-judge quality scores are systematically inflated by output length: longer answers win more regardless of added value, and this length bias is far stronger than in human raters. They fit a GLM predicting the judge's preference from mediators (notably length difference) and then evaluate the counterfactual 'what if both outputs were the same length?', which raises Spearman correlation with Chatbot Arena from 0.94 to 0.98 and makes the metric far harder to game by padding.  
  **Use.** Directly defuses the verbosity trap in the quality half of any novelty score. Because a 'diverse' system can game judge-based quality (and even originality, via more n-grams) simply by writing longer, length-control every quality measurement: regress out length and report length-controlled scores, or hold token budget fixed across systems. Without this, a divergence benchmark can show fake gains that are pure verbosity.  
  *Verified via arxiv id_list lookup 2404.04475.*
- ✅ [Texygen: A Benchmarking Platform for Text Generation Models (introduces Self-BLEU)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.01886) — *Yaoming Zhu, Sidi Lu, Lei Zheng, Jiaxian Guo, Weinan Zhang, Jun Wang, Yong Yu* (2018, arXiv:1802.01886)  
  **Finding.** Establishes the standard lexical diversity metrics — Self-BLEU (average BLEU of each generation against all others; lower = more diverse) alongside distinct-n — and pairs them with quality metrics (BLEU/NLL) so quality and diversity are reported jointly rather than in isolation. The platform's core methodological point: a model can win on quality while collapsing on diversity (and vice versa), so any single-axis score is misleading.  
  **Use.** Use Self-BLEU and distinct-n only as cheap, transparent SURFACE-level sanity checks / lower bounds, never as the headline. They are useful precisely because they are easy to audit and hard to fake without real lexical change — but they reward paraphrase and miss semantic duplication, so they must sit underneath the semantic metrics (NoveltyBench clusters, Vendi). Report them to show the diversity gain isn't an artifact of one metric's blind spot.  
  *Verified via arxiv id_list lookup 1802.01886.*
- ✅ [Justice or Prejudice? Quantifying Biases in LLM-as-a-Judge](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02736) — *Jiayi Ye, Yanbo Wang, Yue Huang, Dongping Chen, Qihui Zhang, Nuno Moniz, Tian Gao, Werner Geyer, Chao Huang, Pin-Yu Chen, Nitesh V. Chawla, Xiangliang Zhang* (2024, arXiv:2410.02736)  
  **Finding.** Systematically catalogs and quantifies 12 judge biases (verbosity, position/order, self-preference/egocentric, authority, sentiment, bandwagon, distraction, etc.) with an automated framework (CALM) that perturbs inputs to measure each bias without human labels. Shows even strong judges have measurable, exploitable biases — including self-preference, where a judge favors text from its own model family.  
  **Use.** Defines the audit checklist for the quality half of a novelty benchmark. Avoid grading-your-own-homework (self-preference) by never using the same model family as generator and judge; randomize/swap positions to kill order bias; run CALM-style perturbation probes (e.g., inject authority cues, vary length) to confirm your judge's quality scores track substance, not style. Pair with length-control (2404.04475) to neutralize the strongest single bias.  
  *Verified via arxiv id_list lookup 2410.02736.*


## LLM-as-judge biases and how to neutralize them

**Recommended protocol**

1. 1. Define the target precisely and operationally. 'Novelty' = divergence from the OBVIOUS/modal answer to the same prompt, not raw difference. Construct an explicit reference 'obvious answer' per prompt (e.g. greedy/low-temperature baseline output, or the most common human response) so novelty is measured as distance FROM that baseline, not in a vacuum.
2. 2. Keep the judge independent from the generator. The judge model must be a different family than any system being scored, and must never judge its own outputs (G-Eval self-reinforcement; Self-Preference Bias). If your pipeline uses an LLM to select/optimize outputs, the final-score judge must be a different lineage than that selector.
3. 3. Use a structured, rubric-based, evidence-first judge (G-Eval style). Force the judge to write its reasoning and fill separate sub-scores — non-obviousness, semantic distance from the baseline, surprise/usefulness — BEFORE emitting any number, so it can't post-hoc rationalize a slot or length preference.
4. 4. Run pairwise with full counterbalancing. Compare your-system vs baseline in BOTH A/B orders; a 'more novel' verdict counts only if it survives the swap (MT-Bench / Not-Fair-Evaluators). Order-dependent verdicts are logged as no-decision and reported as a flip-rate, not broken silently.
5. 5. Use a panel of ≥3 independent judges from different families (LiveIdeaBench) and aggregate. Report inter-judge agreement; gate confidence on it. Disagreement among judges on a pair means 'unreliable', not 'average it and move on'.
6. 6. Control verbosity as a causal confound. Log token length for every output and fit a length-controlled GLM (AlpacaEval); report the novelty effect AFTER partialling out length. Additionally run a matched-length and an inflated-verbosity variant of your own system as an adversarial check.
7. 7. Control familiarity/perplexity. Log the judge's log-prob/perplexity over each candidate; check (and regress out) whether the novelty score is merely tracking low perplexity. Genuinely divergent output is high-perplexity, so verify the judge isn't penalizing it (Self-Preference Bias).
8. 8. Measure judge reliability explicitly before reporting any novelty number: Repetition Stability, Position Consistency, and Preference Fairness (Judging the Judges) — and report them SEPARATELY for near-tie pairs, where bias concentrates.
9. 9. Anchor to humans. Hand-label a stratified subset for novelty (multiple human raters, report Cohen's/Krippendorff agreement). The automated judge is only trusted on the axes/regimes where it reaches human-human agreement levels (~80%, MT-Bench); elsewhere fall back to human-in-the-loop (Not-Fair-Evaluators entropy routing).
10. 10. Pre-register and ablate. Fix prompts, rubric, judge set, and decision rule before seeing results. Report the full bias panel (length-controlled delta, perplexity correlation, swap flip-rate, inter-judge agreement, human correlation) alongside the headline novelty score — never the headline alone.

**Honesty traps (how this measurement can lie) & fixes**

- Grading your own homework (self-preference/self-enhancement): if the judge is the same model/family that generated or selected the outputs, the score measures self-resemblance, not novelty. Avoid: independent judge family, judge never sees its own outputs, panel of ≥3 distinct families (MT-Bench; Self-Preference Bias; LiveIdeaBench).
- Familiarity inversion — the novelty-specific trap: LLM judges over-reward LOW-perplexity, on-distribution text and under-reward the high-perplexity text that genuine divergence produces, so a naive judge systematically scores fluent paraphrase as 'novel' and real outside-the-box output as 'bad'. Avoid: log judge perplexity per candidate, regress it out, and report novelty-score-vs-perplexity correlation (Self-Preference Bias, 2410.21819).
- Verbosity inflation: novel outputs tend to be longer (more ideas), and judges reward length per se, so the 'novelty win' is partly just more text. Avoid: length-controlled GLM, matched-length re-runs, and an inflated-verbosity adversarial baseline (AlpacaEval, 2404.04475).
- Position/order gaming: a verdict that flips when you swap A/B is noise dressed as signal; a weak system can be made to 'win' just by slot placement. Avoid: counterbalanced both-orders judging, require swap-consistency, report flip-rate, escalate order-dependent items to humans (Not-Fair-Evaluators, 2305.17926).
- Lexical/paraphrase metrics rewarding surface change: BLEU/ROUGE/embedding-distance reward outputs that are lexically different from a reference even when semantically identical — i.e. they reward paraphrase and synonym-swapping as 'divergence'. Avoid: never use n-gram overlap as a novelty proxy; measure semantic distance from the OBVIOUS answer plus human-anchored judgment.
- Difference ≠ novelty: a judge can be steered to reward weird/wrong/incoherent outputs as 'creative'. Avoid: score originality jointly with validity/usefulness as separate rubric dimensions so nonsense can't win the novelty axis (LiveIdeaBench dimensions; G-Eval form-filling).
- Reporting only the easy cases: position and self-preference bias concentrate on near-tie comparisons — exactly the 'is this genuinely more novel?' regime — so an aggregate consistency number borrowed from easy, lopsided pairs hides the real unreliability. Avoid: stratify and report reliability metrics on near-tie pairs specifically (Judging the Judges, 2406.07791).
- Optimization loop / self-reinforcement: if the same judge both selects/trains outputs AND scores final novelty, the benchmark measures over-fit to the judge, not real divergence, and the number inflates over iterations. Avoid: strict separation of selector vs evaluator lineage; hold out an independent final judge (G-Eval warning, 2303.16634).
- Prompt leakage of the desired verdict: asking 'how novel and impressive is this output?' primes a high score. Avoid: neutral, blinded, rubric-anchored prompts; blind the judge to which system produced which answer; pre-register the rubric.
- Skipping the human anchor: a high automated novelty score with no human-labeled calibration is unfalsifiable. Avoid: report human inter-rater agreement and judge-vs-human correlation on a stratified subset, and trust the automated judge only where it matches human-human agreement (MT-Bench ~80%).

- ✅ [Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685) — *Lianmin Zheng, Wei-Lin Chiang, Ying Sheng, Siyuan Zhuang, Zhanghao Wu, Yonghao Zhuang, Zi Lin, Zhuohan Li, Dacheng Li, Eric P. ## Compute-fair comparison & pass@k / coverage for problem-solving

**Recommended protocol**

1. 1. Pick verifiable tasks first. Choose problems with a programmatic correctness oracle (unit tests, formal checker, exact numeric/string match) and, ideally, multiple distinct accepted solutions so 'novel-but-correct' is detectable. Avoid small-answer-space tasks (MCQ, single-integer math) where guessing dominates (Yona et al.).
2. 2. Fix a compute budget as the primary X-axis. Measure total cost (FLOPs or tokens or wall-clock or $) per problem, charging the divergence engine for ALL rollouts + search + verifier calls. Define matched budgets B1<B2<... (Snell et al.).
3. 3. Build compute-matched baselines, not single-shot strawmen: (a) the single-pass model at temperature 0; (b) best-of-N of the SAME base model at equal FLOPs to the engine; (c) optionally a larger model at equal FLOPs. The engine must beat best-of-N-at-equal-budget, not just pass@1.
4. 4. Add a dumb-guessing control: enumerate the k most frequent training-set answers (or k random plausible answers) and compute its pass@k, to prove coverage gains are not just answer-prior exploitation (Yona et al.).
5. 5. Sample n >> k per problem (e.g. n=200) for every system and compute the UNBIASED pass@k estimator pass@k = E[1 - C(n-c,k)/C(n,k)] with per-problem bootstrap CIs (Chen et al.).
6. 6. Report the FULL pass@k / coverage curve out to large k (hundreds to thousands), not just pass@1. The decisive comparison is whether the engine raises the large-k frontier vs the base model — if the base catches up at large k, the engine only improves selection, not capability (Yue et al. / 2504.13837).
7. 7. Separate coverage from selected accuracy. Report (a) pass@k = does the correct/novel answer ever appear, and (b) accuracy under a REALISTIC non-oracle selector (majority vote, reward model, the engine's own picker). Claim usefulness only if (b) beats baselines at matched compute (Brown et al.).
8. 8. Measure novelty/diversity directly, not via an LLM judge: distinct correct-solution count, semantic/AST clustering of correct solutions, self-BLEU or embedding-distance among samples, and 'new solutions found that the baseline never samples in N tries' (the true outside-the-box signal).
9. 9. Stratify all results by problem difficulty (baseline success-rate bins). Divergence engines help most on medium-hard, non-trivial problems and waste compute on the hardest; a single aggregate hides this (Snell et al.).
10. 10. If any LLM-judge scoring is unavoidable: length-control it, use a judge from a different model family than the system under test, blind and order-randomize, and validate against a human-labeled subset (Wataoka et al.).
11. 11. Pre-register the metric, k values, budgets, decoding params (temperature/top-p), and seeds; release samples so pass@k is reproducible. Hold out a fresh test set to rule out contamination of the 'novel' answers.

**Honesty traps (how this measurement can lie) & fixes**

- Coverage-without-a-picker: reporting pass@k / coverage as if it were accuracy. Coverage is an upper bound that only materializes with a verifier; verifier-free selection plateaus while coverage climbs. FIX: always pair pass@k with selected-accuracy under a realistic non-oracle selector (Brown et al.).
- Unmatched compute: comparing a 200-sample + search + verifier engine against one greedy decode. FIX: charge the engine for all rollouts/search/verifier FLOPs and give the baseline an equal-budget best-of-N (and/or a larger model at equal FLOPs) (Snell et al.).
- Small-k myopia: declaring victory at pass@1 while the base model overtakes at large k, meaning the engine only re-weighted existing solutions and may have NARROWED the support. FIX: report the full pass@k curve to large k; novelty requires raising the large-k frontier, not pass@1 (Yue et al.).
- Skewed-answer-space guessing: rising coverage that is really 'enumerate the popular answers.' FIX: include a frequency-enumeration / random-guess control and prefer large-answer-space, free-form tasks (Yona et al.).
- Naive/biased pass@k estimator: 'did any of k pass' is biased and high-variance and lets cherry-picked seeds inflate scores. FIX: unbiased combinatorial estimator with n>>k and bootstrap CIs (Chen et al.).
- LLM-judge verbosity & self-preference bias: grading 'novelty/quality' with a judge rewards longer answers and the system's own style, so a verbose engine 'wins' without being better. FIX: prefer verifiable oracles; if judging, length-control, cross-family judge, blind/randomize, validate vs humans (Wataoka et al.).
- Grading your own homework: using the same model (or family) as both generator and judge/verifier/reward model inflates scores via self-preference and shared blind spots. FIX: independent verifier (tests/formal) and a different-family judge.
- Novelty theater: 'diverse' outputs that are paraphrases or wrong. Lexical diversity metrics reward surface variation; an output can be novel and useless. FIX: require correctness via oracle, then measure diversity ONLY among correct solutions (distinct-solution count, semantic/AST clusters).
- Oracle leakage / contamination: counting a 'novel' solution the model memorized from training, or letting the verifier leak the answer into selection. FIX: fresh held-out test set, contamination checks, and keep the selector blind to ground truth.
- Aggregate-hiding: a single headline number averaging easy and impossible problems hides that gains come only from a difficulty band. FIX: stratify by baseline difficulty and report per-bin deltas.
- Seed/temperature cherry-picking and non-reproducibility: tuning decoding to flatter the engine. FIX: pre-register decoding params, k, budgets, seeds, and release raw samples.

  **Finding.** Introduces the pass@k metric AND its unbiased estimator.
